SPD Geometry: Motivation and Core Properties#

Why Covariance Matrices and SPD Geometry?#

Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) matrices naturally arise in many domains as covariance matrices, correlation matrices, and kernel matrices. Unlike general matrices, SPD matrices form a special geometric structure—a Riemannian manifold—with unique properties that can be exploited for better machine learning models.

The key insight is that SPD matrices do not form a vector space. Operations like addition or standard Euclidean distance can produce meaningless results or even invalid matrices (e.g., losing positive definiteness). By respecting the manifold structure, we can:

  • Preserve positive definiteness during optimization

  • Compute meaningful distances and interpolations

  • Leverage geometric invariances (affine invariance, inversion invariance)

  • Build more robust statistical models

Core Geometric Properties#

SPD matrices live on a curved manifold denoted as \(\mathcal{S}_{++}^n\), the space of \(n \times n\) symmetric positive definite matrices. This manifold has several important properties:

Riemannian Metric#

The manifold is equipped with a Riemannian metric that defines distances and angles. A common choice is the affine-invariant metric (also called the Fisher-Rao metric), which ensures that distances are invariant under congruence transformations:

(1)#\[d(\mathbf{P}_1, \mathbf{P}_2) = \left\| \log(\mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2} \mathbf{P}_2 \mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2}) \right\|_F\]

where \(\|\cdot\|_F\) denotes the Frobenius norm.

Geodesics and Interpolation#

On the SPD manifold, the shortest path between two points (geodesic) is not a straight line but follows the curved geometry. For SPD matrices \(\mathbf{P}_1\) and \(\mathbf{P}_2\), the geodesic can be computed as:

(2)#\[\gamma(t) = \mathbf{P}_1^{1/2} \left( \mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2} \mathbf{P}_2 \mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2} \right)^t \mathbf{P}_1^{1/2}, \quad t \in [0, 1]\]

This provides a natural way to interpolate between covariance matrices.

Example: Computing a Geodesic#

import torch
from spd_learn.functional import (
    geodesic_interpolation_spdairm,
    geodesic_distance_spdairm,
)

# Two SPD matrices (e.g., covariance matrices from different conditions)
P1 = torch.tensor([[2.0, 0.5], [0.5, 1.0]])
P2 = torch.tensor([[1.0, 0.3], [0.3, 2.0]])

# Compute geodesic at t=0.5 (midpoint)
midpoint = geodesic_interpolation_spdairm(P1, P2, t=0.5)

# Compute Riemannian distance
dist = geodesic_distance_spdairm(P1, P2)
print(f"Riemannian distance: {dist.item():.4f}")

What Advantages Does This Provide?#

Understanding these core geometric properties sets the foundation for the more detailed discussion of inherent properties and their exploitation in the next section (Inherent Properties and Their Exploitation).

Key advantages include:

  1. Invariance Properties: The affine-invariant metric is invariant to linear transformations, making it robust to changes in coordinate systems or units of measurement.

  2. Positive Definiteness Preservation: All operations (mean, interpolation, etc.) remain on the manifold, ensuring valid covariance matrices.

  3. Statistical Efficiency: Riemannian methods often require fewer samples to achieve the same performance as Euclidean approaches.

  4. Natural Geometry: The manifold structure aligns with the natural geometry of the data, leading to better generalization in machine learning tasks.

Key Takeaways#

  • SPD matrices form a curved manifold, not a vector space

  • Respecting the geometry leads to invariant, meaningful operations

  • Geodesics provide natural interpolation and distance measures

  • These properties can be exploited for more robust machine learning models

For a detailed exploration of these properties and practical examples of how to exploit them in neuroimaging applications, see Inherent Properties and Their Exploitation.
