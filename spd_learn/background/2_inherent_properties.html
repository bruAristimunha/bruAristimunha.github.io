Inherent Properties and Their Exploitation#

The SPD manifold structure offers several unique properties that can be exploited for neuroimaging analysis. Understanding these properties is crucial for building effective machine learning models.

Geodesic Completeness and Boundary Avoidance#

Unlike Euclidean space, where interpolation or optimization can produce rank-deficient matrices at finite distances, the affine-invariant and log-Euclidean Riemannian metrics ensure geodesic completeness:

  • Boundary avoidance: Geodesics between SPD matrices never reach boundary points (matrices with zero eigenvalues remain infinitely distant)

  • Intrinsic optimization: Iterative updates during gradient descent stay strictly within the SPD manifold, preventing invalid intermediate results

This property resolves critical limitations in conventional tensor field processing, where operations like interpolation, regularization, or noise filtering often produce non-SPD matrices.

Mathematical Formulation#

For any two SPD matrices \(\mathbf{P}_1, \mathbf{P}_2 \in \mathcal{S}_{++}^n\), the geodesic connecting them is given by:

(1)#\[\gamma(t) = \mathbf{P}_1^{1/2} \left( \mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2} \mathbf{P}_2 \mathbf{P}_1^{-1/2} \right)^t \mathbf{P}_1^{1/2}, \quad t \in [0, 1]\]

For all \(t \in [0, 1]\), \(\gamma(t)\) remains strictly SPD—never approaching the boundary where eigenvalues would be zero.

Example: Geodesic Completeness#

import torch
from spd_learn.functional import geodesic_interpolation_spdairm

# Two SPD matrices with very different eigenvalue scales
P1 = torch.tensor([[100.0, 0.0], [0.0, 0.01]])  # Large condition number
P2 = torch.tensor([[1.0, 0.5], [0.5, 1.0]])

# Interpolate along geodesic - all points remain SPD
t_values = torch.linspace(0, 1, 10)
for t in t_values:
    P_t = geodesic_interpolation_spdairm(P1, P2, t=t.item())
    eigvals = torch.linalg.eigvalsh(P_t)
    print(f"t={t:.2f}: min eigenvalue = {eigvals[0]:.6f} (always > 0)")

Affine Invariance and Robustness#

The affine-invariant metric exhibits congruence invariance, a critical property for neuroimaging analysis:

(2)#\[d(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P}_1\mathbf{A}^\top, \mathbf{A}\mathbf{P}_2\mathbf{A}^\top) = d(\mathbf{P}_1, \mathbf{P}_2)\]

for any invertible matrix \(\mathbf{A}\).

Why This Matters for EEG/MEG#

In EEG/MEG analysis, the observed signals are linear mixtures of neural sources:

(3)#\[\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{s}(t)\]

where \(\mathbf{A}\) is the spatial mixing matrix (electrode/sensor positions, head geometry, conductivity) and \(\mathbf{s}(t)\) are the neural sources.

The problem: \(\mathbf{A}\) varies across:

  • Different subjects (individual head anatomy)

  • Different sessions (electrode repositioning)

  • Different experimental setups (cap placement, impedance changes)

The solution: The Riemannian distance between covariance matrices remains consistent despite these variations, making classification and analysis robust to session/subject-specific mixing matrices.

Example: Affine Invariance#

import torch
from spd_learn.functional import geodesic_distance_spdairm

# Original covariance matrices
P1 = torch.tensor([[2.0, 0.5], [0.5, 1.5]])
P2 = torch.tensor([[1.5, 0.3], [0.3, 2.0]])

# Compute original distance
dist_original = geodesic_distance_spdairm(P1, P2)

# Apply arbitrary linear transformation (simulate subject/session change)
A = torch.randn(2, 2)
P1_transformed = A @ P1 @ A.T
P2_transformed = A @ P2 @ A.T

# Distance is preserved!
dist_transformed = geodesic_distance_spdairm(P1_transformed, P2_transformed)

print(f"Original distance: {dist_original.item():.6f}")
print(f"Transformed distance: {dist_transformed.item():.6f}")
print(f"Difference: {abs(dist_original - dist_transformed).item():.2e}")

Outlier Resistance of Geometric Mean#

The Riemannian (Fréchet) mean demonstrates inherent resistance to outliers compared to the arithmetic mean. In both Gaussian and chi-square distributions, the geometric mean deviates less significantly from distribution centers, making it more robust for neuroimaging applications where artifacts and noise are common.

Geometric vs. Arithmetic Mean#

For a set of SPD matrices \(\{\mathbf{P}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_N\}\):

Arithmetic mean (Euclidean):

(4)#\[\bar{\mathbf{P}}_{\text{arith}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{P}_i\]

Geometric mean (Riemannian):

(5)#\[\bar{\mathbf{P}}_{\text{geom}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{P}} \sum_{i=1}^N d^2(\mathbf{P}, \mathbf{P}_i)\]

The geometric mean is less influenced by outliers because extreme values contribute less to the minimization in the curved geometry of the manifold.

Example: Outlier Resistance#

import torch
from spd_learn.functional import frechet_mean

# Generate a set of "normal" covariance matrices
normal_covs = []
for _ in range(50):
    A = torch.randn(5, 5)
    cov = A @ A.T + torch.eye(5)
    normal_covs.append(cov)

# Add an outlier (very large eigenvalues)
outlier = torch.eye(5) * 100.0
all_covs = normal_covs + [outlier]

# Compute both means
all_covs_tensor = torch.stack(all_covs)

# Arithmetic mean (affected by outlier)
mean_arith = all_covs_tensor.mean(dim=0)

# Geometric (Riemannian) mean (robust to outlier)
mean_geom = frechet_mean(all_covs_tensor)

print(f"Arithmetic mean trace: {torch.trace(mean_arith).item():.2f}")
print(f"Geometric mean trace: {torch.trace(mean_geom).item():.2f}")
print("Geometric mean is less affected by the outlier!")

From Neuroimaging Signals to SPD Matrices#

In practice, covariance matrices are constructed from time-series neuroimaging data. Understanding this process clarifies how SPD geometry applies to real-world signals.

Covariance from Fourier Coefficients#

For frequency-specific analysis (e.g., EEG motor imagery), signals are often bandpass-filtered and Fourier-transformed. Let \(\mathbf{Z}_f^{(i)}\) denote the complex Fourier coefficients at frequency \(f\) for trial \(i\). The spatial covariance matrix is derived as:

(6)#\[\mathbf{C}_f^{(i)} = \Re\!\left( \mathbf{Z}_f^{(i)} \, \overline{\mathbf{Z}_f^{(i)}}^{\,\top} \right)\]

where \(\overline{\mathbf{Z}_f^{(i)}}^{\,\top}\) denotes the conjugate transpose and \(\Re\) extracts the real part. This produces an SPD matrix capturing spatial dependencies at frequency \(f\).

Connection to Classical Features#

The diagonal elements of \(\mathbf{C}_f^{(i)}\) correspond to the power spectral density (PSD) at each channel. The classical log-PSD features can be extracted as:

(7)#\[\log\mathrm{PSD}^{(i)} = \log\!\big[\, \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{C}_1^{(i)})^{\top}, \ldots, \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{C}_F^{(i)})^{\top} \,\big]\]

Key insight: The full covariance matrix \(\mathbf{C}_f^{(i)}\) contains not only the diagonal PSD values but also the off-diagonal cross-channel correlations. SPD geometry allows us to leverage this complete second-order structure, which encodes functional connectivity patterns that are lost when using only diagonal elements.

This is identical to the log-diagonal vectorization used for predictive modeling, but SPD methods work with the full matrix structure rather than discarding the off-diagonal information.

Example: Covariance Construction from EEG#

import torch
import numpy as np
from scipy.signal import stft

# Simulate EEG data: (n_channels, n_timepoints)
eeg_data = torch.randn(32, 1000)  # 32 channels, 1000 time points

# Apply bandpass filter (e.g., 8-13 Hz for alpha band)
# [Filtering code omitted for brevity]

# Compute Short-Time Fourier Transform
f, t, Zxx = stft(eeg_data.numpy(), fs=250, nperseg=128)

# Select frequency band of interest (e.g., alpha: 8-13 Hz)
freq_idx = np.where((f >= 8) & (f <= 13))[0]
Z_alpha = torch.from_numpy(Zxx[:, freq_idx, :])

# Compute covariance for each time window
n_channels = Z_alpha.shape[0]
n_times = Z_alpha.shape[2]

covariances = []
for t_idx in range(n_times):
    Z_t = Z_alpha[:, :, t_idx]  # Shape: (n_channels, n_freqs)
    # Covariance: C = Re(Z * Z^H)
    C = torch.real(Z_t @ Z_t.conj().T)
    covariances.append(C)

# Stack into batch of SPD matrices
C_batch = torch.stack(covariances)  # Shape: (n_times, n_channels, n_channels)

print(f"Covariance batch shape: {C_batch.shape}")

# Extract log-PSD (diagonal elements only - classical approach)
log_psd = torch.log(torch.diagonal(C_batch, dim1=1, dim2=2))
print(f"Log-PSD shape: {log_psd.shape}")
print(f"Information used: Diagonal only ({log_psd.numel()} features)")

# Or work with full SPD matrices using Riemannian geometry
from spd_learn.functional import frechet_mean

mean_cov = frechet_mean(C_batch)
print(f"Riemannian mean covariance shape: {mean_cov.shape}")
print(f"Information used: Full matrix ({mean_cov.numel()} features)")

Summary of Advantages#

The inherent properties of SPD manifolds provide concrete advantages for neuroimaging analysis:

  1. Geodesic Completeness: Ensures all optimization and interpolation operations stay within valid SPD space, preventing numerical issues and invalid matrices.

  2. Affine Invariance: Provides robustness to session-specific and subject-specific variations in the spatial mixing matrix—critical for EEG/MEG where electrode placement varies.

  3. Outlier Resistance: The geometric mean naturally resists artifacts and noise, common in real-world neuroimaging data.

  4. Complete Second-Order Information: Full covariance matrices capture both power (diagonal) and synchronization (off-diagonal) patterns, unlike classical methods that discard cross-channel correlations.

  5. Statistical Efficiency: Riemannian methods often require fewer samples to achieve good performance—valuable when data acquisition is expensive.

  6. Natural Geometry: The manifold structure aligns with the intrinsic geometry of covariance data, leading to better generalization in machine learning tasks.

Application Context: Why These Properties Matter#

EEG/MEG/ECoG: Spatial covariance matrices capture event-related desynchronization/synchronization effects that characterize sensorimotor rhythms during motor imagery. The affine-invariant metric’s congruence invariance handles subject-specific and session-specific variations in the spatial mixing matrix, while the geometric mean resists bio-artifacts and environmental noise.

fMRI: Functional connectivity matrices (connectomes) encode temporal correlations between BOLD signals across brain regions. The complete second-order structure captures not just regional activity (diagonal) but also functional coupling (off-diagonal), essential for characterizing disorders and cognitive states.

DTI: Diffusion tensors within each voxel form tensor fields. Geodesic completeness prevents invalid intermediate results during anatomical smoothing, fiber tractography, and disease biomarker extraction—operations where Euclidean methods fail by producing non-SPD matrices.

Key Takeaways#

  • Geodesic completeness ensures optimization never leaves the SPD manifold

  • Affine invariance provides robustness to coordinate system changes and mixing matrix variations

  • Geometric mean resists outliers better than arithmetic mean

  • Full covariance matrices encode both power and synchronization patterns

  • SPD geometry exploits complete second-order statistics beyond diagonal PSD features

  • These properties directly address neuroimaging challenges: low SNR, non-stationarity, artifacts, and limited sample sizes
